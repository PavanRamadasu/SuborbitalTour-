SpaceShipOne (SS1)

SpaceShipOne (SS1) is a three-person, high-altitude research rocket designed for sub-orbital flights. It was the first private crewed space vehicle.
SpaceShipOne was designed by Burt Rutan's Scaled Composites to win the Ansari X Prize. It was developed by Mojave Aerospace Ventures, a joint venture between Paul Allen and Scaled Composites. The ship was launched without government funding.

SpaceShipOne's features include:

  • Configuration: It can transform into three different configurations during flight.
  • Flight altitude: It can fly to 100 km altitude.
  • Re-entry: It can convert to a stable, high-drag shape for atmospheric entry. This configuration reduces aero/thermal loads and allows for a "hands-off" re-entry.

On June 21, 2004, SpaceShipOne made the first privately funded human spaceflight. It flew past the boundary of space (100,000 meters, or 328,000 feet) over the United States.

SpaceShipTwo (SS2)

SpaceShipTwo (SS2) is a suborbital spaceplane that can launch eight astronauts into space. It's the world's largest aircraft made of carbon composite.

Here's some information about SS2:

  • Speed: SS2 can reach 2,600 mph (4,200 km/h).
  • Altitude: SS2 launches from its mother ship, White Knight Two, at an altitude of 13,500 m (44,300 ft).
  • Lift capacity: SS2 has a unique high altitude lift capacity.
  • Wings: SS2's wings can pivot to a feathered position, which makes it aerodynamically stable and automatically positions it for re-entry into the atmosphere.
  • Design: SS2 is a descendant of the SpaceShipOne design, which was the first privately designed and owned vehicle to fly into space and return.

Virgin Galactic started selling tickets for flights on SS2 in 2005 for an initial price of $200,000. Today, the price has increased to $450,000.

Suborbital Space

Suborbital space tourism offers a limited view of Earth from space. Suborbital vehicles can only see a few thousand kilometers of land or sea around their spaceports. They can also see a few hundred kilometers of land or sea directly below them
Suborbital space tourism involves rocket-powered flights that take tourists beyond Earth's atmosphere but not into orbit. Suborbital flights are offered by Virgin Galactic and Blue Origin.
Suborbital flights are more accessible than orbital flights because they are shorter and at lower altitudes. Passengers experience a few minutes of weightlessness and a brief view of Earth from a high altitude.

Budget for Suborbital Space Tour

Suborbital space trips can cost between $200,000 and $500,000.
This includes a 15-minute ride to the edge of space and back.
Flights to orbit are much more expensive, costing over $50 million per seat.

Here are some suborbital space trip costs:

  • Blue Origin: $200,000–$300,000 per person
  • Virgin Galactic's SpaceShipTwo: $250,000–$500,000 per seat
  • Blue Origin's orbital launch services: $50 million–$100 million per person

Some factors that could make space tourism more affordable include:

  • Reusable rocket technology
  • Increased competition
  • Economies of scale
  • Technological advancements
